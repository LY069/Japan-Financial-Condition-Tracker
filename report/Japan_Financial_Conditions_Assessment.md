**Prepared for:** investment professionals and policymakers

> **Labelling caveat.** The composite Financial Conditions Index (FCI) below is an *analytical synthesis* on the axes of the BoJ's framework — a z-score composite assembled by this tracker. It is **not** an official BoJ index; the BoJ publishes no single headline FCI and sets out only a *qualitative* comprehensive judgment. "The BoJ's own read" refers to that qualitative judgment; numerical scores are the tracker's measure. Each accommodation score is signed so **positive = more accommodative**; "direction" is the trailing 6-month change (▲ easing / ▼ tightening / → stable).

## 1. Executive summary

- **Financial conditions remain accommodative at a 1.00% policy rate.** The composite FCI reads **+0.80** (accommodative), and **four of five framework axes are accommodative**: real rates **+0.19**, availability **+1.09**, asset prices **+3.05**, funding volumes **+0.55**; only **funding costs (−0.89)** is restrictive. This corroborates the BoJ's qualitative conclusion that conditions "remained accommodative even after" the rate hikes.

- **Both lenses agree — accommodation — with caveats.** On the **natural-rate lens**, the real policy rate (**−0.98%**) sits **below the BoJ's real six-model natural-rate band** (latest **−0.94% / −0.36% / +0.53%** for low/mid/high), ~**0.62pp below the midpoint** and even ~0.04pp below the lower bound. On the **financial-conditions lens**, the multi-axis read is clearly easy. Same direction from two independent angles raises confidence in the *sign* even where the *magnitude* is uncertain.

- **The real-rate verdict now uses the longest available history (a fairness fix).** The headline real-rate axis is scored on an **ex-post basis (nominal − realized core CPI) over each maturity's full history** (1Y/3Y from 1974, 10Y from 1986): **+0.19, mildly accommodative**. The **2005+ ex-ante** read (**−0.26, "restrictive"**) is retained only as a *reference* — and is biased tight because its baseline is dominated by Japan's zero/negative-rate era (see §3, §4.1).

- **Funding costs are the one tightening axis — but it's safe-rate repricing, not credit stress.** Lending and CP issuance rates have risen with the policy rate (avg new-loan rate **1.17%**, +0.22pp y/y; CP **1.11%**, +0.42pp y/y), yet the **AA corporate-bond spread is just 0.31%** (≈ flat) and scores *accommodative*. The cost rise is the risk-free base, not widening risk premia. (This axis is scored vs 2005+ history and so carries the same tight bias — see §4.2.)

- **Accommodation is narrowing but has stabilized recently.** The FCI eased from **+1.00 (Dec-2024)** to **+0.80 (Jun-2026)** as the hikes fed through; over the last six months it is broadly **stable**. The real-policy-rate-vs-r\* gap has closed from **−1.44pp (Dec-2024) to −0.62pp** — clearly tightening, yet still accommodative.

- **Near-term scope for further gradual hikes exists, but is bounded.** With the real policy rate below the natural-rate band and conditions still easy, the data are *consistent with* continued, data-dependent normalization toward the **lower end** of the neutral band. Binding constraints: a fast-rising long-end (10Y JGB **2.65%**, +0.59pp y/y; 30Y **3.80%**), a **weak yen (USD/JPY ~161)**, U.S./global spillovers to Japan's r\*, SME/high-debt-sector stress, and wide r\* uncertainty itself.

**Bottom line:** *accommodative, narrowing, with room for cautious further normalization.*

## 2. The BoJ framework

BoJ Review 2026-E-4 makes one central methodological point: because the natural rate of interest (r\*) "cannot be pinned down in advance" and "must be viewed with considerable latitude," the Bank does **not** mechanically set policy off any r\* estimate. Instead it makes a **comprehensive judgment** over economic activity, prices, and — critically — **financial conditions**, which are the *transmission channel* from monetary policy to the real economy. The Fed, ECB and BoE are quoted taking the same stance ("navigating by the stars under cloudy skies").

The Bank assesses financial conditions in **two stages** (Chart 6 of the paper):

| Stage | What it captures | Indicators in this tracker |
|---|---|---|
| **Stage 1 — Interest-rate developments** | The *impulse*: short policy rate and safe-asset (JGB) yields, and especially **real** rates by maturity (real rates drive spending/investment) | Policy rate; JGB 1/2/5/10/30Y; real rates by maturity (1Y/3Y/10Y) |
| **Stage 2 — Funding environment** | The *response*: has the impulse changed how cheaply, easily and how much the economy funds itself? | **Funding costs** (lending & CP rates, corp-bond spread); **availability** (Tankan lending-attitude & financial-position DIs); **asset prices** (equities, FX); **funding volumes** (bank lending, CP & corporate-bond outstanding) |

The logic is sequential: a hike has only "bitten" if the Stage-1 impulse shows through into higher funding **costs and** tighter availability **and** slowing volumes (Stage 2). When availability stays easy and volumes keep growing after a hike, the BoJ infers the change in the *degree* of accommodation has been small.

## 4. Current assessment by axis

### 4.1 Stage 1 — interest-rate developments  ·  axis **+0.19 (mildly accommodative, ▲ easing)**

The policy rate has been lifted **0.25 → 0.50 → 0.75 → 1.00%**; the JGB curve has risen across maturities (1Y **1.14%**, 2Y **1.41%**, 5Y **1.94%**, 10Y **2.65%** (+0.59pp y/y), 30Y **3.80%**). The question is the **real** rate, and the answer depends entirely on the baseline:

| Baseline window | Real-rate basis | Axis score | Assessment |
|---|---|---:|---|
| **Full history (1974/86+) — headline** | ex-post (nominal − realized core CPI) | **+0.19** | **Mildly accommodative** |
| Since 1990 | ex-post | −0.03 | Broadly neutral |
| Since 1995 | ex-post | −0.26 | Mildly restrictive |
| Since 2000 | ex-post | −0.35 | Mildly restrictive |
| Since 2005 (ex-ante) — reference \* | ex-ante (nominal − expected inflation) | −0.26 | Mildly restrictive |

> \* **The 2005+ reference is biased toward "restrictive."** Its baseline is dominated by ZIRP (late-1990s on) and NIRP (2016–2024), so the *average* real rate over the window is unusually low; a normalizing real rate therefore scores *above* that depressed mean. It is shown for continuity, not as the headline.

**Why the headline now uses the longest history.** A 2005+ z-score judges today's real rate against ~two decades of near-zero policy — almost guaranteeing that any normalization looks "tight." Extending to each maturity's full history (the only inflation series available that far back is *realized* core CPI, hence ex-post) gives the fairer read: **mildly accommodative (+0.19)**. The verdict is genuinely horizon-dependent — neutral vs 1990, mildly restrictive vs post-1995 — and, importantly, the restrictive readings are **not merely a NIRP artifact**: Japan's deflation episodes (negative CPI in 2009–10 and the mid-2010s) produced *high* ex-post real rates, and the **10Y ex-post real rate (+1.14%) is now back at ~its long-run norm** (z ≈ 0). Latest ex-post real rates by maturity: **1Y −0.37%, 3Y +0.08%, 10Y +1.14%** — the front end still negative (accommodative), the long end normalized. (Use the dashboard's maturity pulldown to see each tenor against its own average ±1σ/±2σ.)

The honest synthesis: **front-end real rates remain accommodative; the long end is roughly neutral; the policy stance overall is still easy on a long-run baseline**, even though it is tighter than the deflation-era norm.

### 4.2 Stage 2 — funding environment  ·  Stage score **+0.95 (accommodative, → stable)**

**Funding costs (axis −0.89, restrictive, ▼ tightening) — the one tightening axis.** The hikes show through here: average contracted new-loan rate **1.17%** (+0.22pp y/y), CP issuance rate **1.11%** (+0.42pp y/y). But the **AA corporate-bond spread is 0.31%** (≈ flat y/y) and scores *accommodative* — so the cost rise is almost entirely the **risk-free base** repricing, **not** credit-risk widening.
> **What "restrictive" is measured against.** This axis is a z-score vs **2005-onward** history — the same ZIRP/NIRP-dominated baseline that biases the 2005 real-rate reference. Because 2005–2024 lending/CP rates were ultra-low, today's ~1.1–1.2% rates score "above the norm" and read restrictive, even though they are low in absolute and long-run terms. Unlike the real-rate axis, these BoJ cost series are not readily available far enough back to re-baseline on a multi-decade window, so the axis stays on 2005+; read it as **"tighter than the deflation-era norm," not "tight in absolute terms."**

**Availability of funds (axis +1.09, accommodative, → stable).** Tankan diffusion indices remain clearly easy: lending-attitude DI **+23.7** (large) / **+17.6** (small); financial-position DI **+21.5** (large) / **+13.7** (small). Banks are willing to lend and firms judge their cash positions comfortable — no sign of a credit squeeze, including for SMEs.

**Asset prices (axis +3.05, accommodative, ▲ easing).** Equities are elevated (TOPIX **~3,960**; Nikkei 225 **~67,400**) and the **yen is weak (USD/JPY ~161)** — both transmit through balance-sheet and expected-return channels. *Caveat:* this axis is the most globally driven; a strong score reflects world risk appetite and the carry trade as much as domestic policy, so weight it less than the domestic gauges.

**Funding volumes (axis +0.55, accommodative, → stable).** The transmission acid test — are firms still borrowing? **Bank lending +3.1% y/y** and **CP + corporate-bond outstanding +6.4% y/y**, both only a touch below a year ago (3.3%, 6.5%). Per the framework, sustained volume growth after a hike implies the change in accommodation has been *small*. **No clear shift in volumes yet** to signal the hikes are biting hard.

## 5. Impact of the BoJ rate hikes (Dec-2025 + Jun-2026)

**Has transmission worked?** Partially, and as expected — strongest at the start of the chain, muted further down:

- **Working channel — funding costs.** Lending rates (+0.22pp y/y) and CP rates (+0.42pp y/y) are passing through. This is transmission doing its job at Stage 2.
- **Long end — repricing fast.** The 10Y JGB is up +0.59pp y/y to 2.65% and the 30Y is at 3.80%; the long-end **real** rate has returned to ~neutral. The curve is steepening (term premium rebuilding as the BoJ steps back from yield control).
- **Muted so far — availability, volumes, assets.** Availability is still easy, lending/issuance volumes are still growing, equities are rich and the yen weak. By the framework's own logic, this means **the change in the degree of accommodation has been modest**.

**The decisive movements** are in **Stage 1 / the policy-rate-vs-r\* gap** (−1.44pp → −0.62pp) and **funding costs** (the only axis to flip restrictive). **Stage 2 as a whole has barely moved** (+0.95) because easy availability, rich assets and growing volumes have cushioned the rise in costs. *The interest-rate impulse is real; the funding-environment response is so far contained.*

**Distributional caveat (per the paper).** A rate rise hits unevenly. SMEs and high-interest-bearing-debt sectors feel it sooner and harder; debt-free, high-profit firms barely. For households, the net effect depends on the saving-vs-borrowing mix and on wages. The aggregate "accommodative" read can therefore mask pockets of genuine tightening — the Tankan small-enterprise DIs (still positive) are the key series to watch here.

## 6. Does the current picture support further rate hikes?

**Reasoned verdict: yes — gradual, data-dependent further normalization toward the lower end of the neutral band is supportable, but the runway is shortening.**

The case **for**:
- The **real policy rate (−0.98%) is below the entire natural-rate band** (even ~0.04pp below the −0.94% lower bound), and ~0.62pp below the midpoint. Even allowing for r\* uncertainty, policy is more likely accommodative than not.
- On the **long-history baseline the real-rate stance is mildly accommodative (+0.19)**, not restrictive.
- **Availability is easy and volumes are growing** — no credit squeeze; the corporate-bond spread (0.31%) shows no risk-premium stress.
- **r\* is drifting up** (potential growth ~0.71%), which widens the room to move.
- Core CPI **~1.5%**, broadly consistent with continued, careful normalization toward 2%.

The case **for caution / against moving fast**:
- **The long end is repricing quickly** (10Y +0.59pp y/y; 30Y 3.80%); the long real rate is already ~neutral, so further front-end hikes risk an outsized curve/term-premium reaction.
- **Weak yen (USD/JPY ~161)** complicates the inflation outlook and is itself a channel.
- **U.S./global spillovers** to Japan's r\* are not well captured by the models.
- **Distributional stress** (SMEs, high-debt sectors) builds with each hike.
- **r\* is imprecise** — the band spans −0.94% to +0.53%; near neutral, the BoJ's own counsel is "considerable latitude."

**Conditional path.** The data support *one or two further 25bp steps* toward the **lower end** of the neutral band, **paced and data-dependent**, with the financial-conditions axes (especially availability, volumes and the corporate-bond spread) as the real-time check on whether accommodation is being withdrawn too fast. A clear roll-over in lending/issuance **volumes**, a downturn in the **Tankan availability DIs**, or a jump in **credit spreads** would be the signal to pause.

## 8. Methodology & data note

**The tracker.** Official-source time series are assembled into the BoJ two-stage framework in a SQLite database (`data/jfct.db`) by an ETL pipeline. Sources: **MoF** (JGB curve), **e-Stat/MIC** (core CPI), **FRED** (policy rate, 10Y JGB, USD/JPY, Nikkei — mirrors of BoJ/MoF/MIC series), and the **BoJ six-model natural-rate estimates** (Chart 3) loaded from the published series; Tankan DIs, the composite inflation-expectations index, lending/CP volumes and equity/FX detail are BoJ-sourced (CSV/seed where no open API exists). Data mode is currently **LIVE**.

**Scoring.** Each indicator → an **accommodation score** = z-score × polarity (so + always = more accommodative). Axis = weight-weighted mean of members (weights reflect activity relevance, e.g. 1Y/3Y real rate 1.0 vs 10Y 0.5; full table in the dashboard footer). Stage = mean of its axes; **composite FCI** = equal-weight mean of the five axes. **Direction** = trailing 6-month change in the score.

**Baseline windows (important).** Most axes are z-scored over **Jan 2005–present**. The **headline real-rate axis is the exception**: it is scored over each maturity's **longest available history** on an **ex-post** basis (nominal − realized core CPI; 1Y/3Y from 1974, 10Y from 1986), because the 2005+ window is dominated by ZIRP/NIRP and biases real-rate (and, similarly, funding-cost) reads toward "restrictive." The 2005+ ex-ante real-rate read is kept as a labelled reference. This mixed-baseline choice is deliberate — real rates have multi-decade official data; the funding-environment gauges mostly do not — and is flagged throughout.

**The composite FCI is an analytical synthesis, not an official BoJ index.** The BoJ publishes no single headline FCI; this operationalizes the paper's qualitative comprehensive judgment. Read it as a structured summary of direction and degree, weighting the underlying axes (the asset-price axis in particular is globally driven).

## Bottom-line verdict

**At a 1.00% policy rate, Japan's financial conditions remain genuinely accommodative (FCI +0.80; real policy rate −0.98%, below the entire BoJ six-model natural-rate band; headline real-rate axis +0.19 on a long-run baseline). Accommodation is narrowing — funding costs have turned restrictive versus the deflation-era norm and the long end has repriced to ~neutral — but availability, volumes and asset prices stay easy and credit spreads show no stress. Both the natural-rate and financial-conditions lenses support further gradual, data-dependent normalization toward the lower end of the neutral band, paced with the BoJ's own "considerable latitude" against a fast-steepening curve, a weak yen, and an uncertain, upward-drifting r\*.**
